1. CPR & AED 2025 AHA/ERC guidelines

1.1 Adult Basic Life Support (BLS) sequence

Scene safety: ensure no danger to rescuer or victim. Check responsiveness: tap and shout. If unresponsive, activate emergency response (or send someone) and get AED.

Breathing check: look for chest rise for ≤10 seconds. If not breathing or only gasping, start CPR.

Chest compressions:

  • Hand placement: lower half of sternum (center of chest).
  • Depth: at least 5 cm (2 inches), not exceeding 6 cm.
  • Rate: 100‑120 per minute.
  • Recoil: allow full chest recoil (do not lean).
  • Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 ventilations (with advanced airway, continuous compressions 1 breath q6s).
  • Minimise interruptions: keep pauses <10 seconds.

Ventilations: head tilt‑chin lift, pinch nose, give 1 second per breath (visible chest rise). Avoid excessive ventilation (increases intrathoracic pressure, reduces venous return).

Cycles: continue 30:2 until AED arrives, victim moves, or EMS takes over.

1.2 AED operation

  • Turn on AED.
  • Attach pads: upper right (below clavicle), lower left (midaxillary, left of nipple). For children, use pediatric pads/attenuator if available; if not, use adult pads (avoid overlap).
  • Ensure no one touches victim during analysis.
  • If shock advised: clear again, press shock button, immediately resume CPR (starting with compressions).
  • If no shock advised: immediately resume CPR.
  • Follow prompts; do not turn off AED.

1.3 Child & infant modifications

Pediatric AED: Use pediatric pads/attenuator if child <8 years or <25 kg. If none, use adult pads (anterior‑posterior placement).

1.4 Opioid‑associated emergency

If unresponsive and likely opioid overdose: give naloxone (intranasal/IM) but do not delay CPR. Rescue breathing is critical – opioids cause respiratory arrest before cardiac arrest.

1.5 Special considerations

  • Pregnancy: manual left uterine displacement (LUD) to relieve aortocaval compression. Prioritize high‑quality compressions.
  • Drowning: give 2 initial rescue breaths before starting compressions.
  • Trauma: immobilize spine if mechanism suggests; jaw thrust instead of head tilt.

2. Choking (Foreign Body Airway Obstruction) FBAO

2.1 Recognition

Universal sign: hands clutching throat. Inability to speak, cough silently, high‑pitched sounds, cyanosis, progressive loss of consciousness.

2.2 Conscious adult/child (>1 year)

  • Ask “Are you choking?” If nods yes and cannot speak, intervene.
  • Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich): stand behind, fist between umbilicus and xiphoid, grasp fist, inward‑upward thrusts. Repeat until object expelled or victim unconscious.
  • If pregnant or obese: use chest thrusts (hand on lower sternum).

2.3 Unconscious adult/child

  • Lower to ground, activate EMS, begin CPR (30:2).
  • Each time airway opens, look for object; if visible, remove with finger sweep (blind sweeps contraindicated).

2.4 Infant (<1 year) – conscious

  • Back blows: 5 firm blows between shoulder blades (head lower than trunk).
  • Chest thrusts: 5 thrusts on sternum (two fingers, same as compressions).
  • Alternate cycles until object expelled or unconscious.

Infant – unconscious: begin CPR (30:2) – compressions may dislodge object.

2.5 Self‑treatment

Use a chair back or counter edge to perform abdominal thrusts on oneself.

3. Wounds & Hemorrhage Control Stop the Bleed

3.1 Types of wounds

  • Abrasion: superficial, epidermal loss.
  • Laceration: tear/ cut (clean vs contaminated).
  • Puncture: deep, high infection risk (tetanus).
  • Avulsion: tissue partially/fully torn off.
  • Amputation: complete separation.

3.2 External hemorrhage control – stepwise

  1. Direct pressure: sterile gauze/cloth, firm pressure. If bleeding soaks through, apply additional dressing – do not remove initial.
  2. Elevation: above heart if no fracture.
  3. Pressure points: if direct pressure insufficient – brachial (arm), femoral (groin).
  4. Hemostatic dressings: (e.g., QuikClot) for severe bleeding not controlled by pressure – pack into wound, hold pressure.
  5. Tourniquet (extremity): place 5‑7 cm proximal to wound (not over joint), tighten until bleeding stops, note time. Do not remove until surgical care. Commercial tourniquets (CAT, SOFTT) preferred over improvised.

Junctional hemorrhage: neck, groin, axilla – cannot use tourniquet; use hemostatic gauze + direct pressure.

3.3 Wound care & infection prevention

  • Irrigate with clean water or saline (high pressure).
  • Remove visible debris; do not scrub.
  • Approximate edges (steristrips) for linear wounds.
  • Cover with sterile dressing; change if saturated.
  • Assess tetanus status: booster if >5 years (dirty wound) or >10 years (clean).

3.4 Internal bleeding – recognition

Signs: tender, swollen, bruised areas (e.g., fractured femur), shock symptoms (pale, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status). Treat for shock: supine, maintain warmth, urgent transport.

4. Fractures & Dislocations orthopedic first aid

4.1 Fracture types

  • Closed: skin intact.
  • Open (compound): bone protrudes or wound communicates with fracture – high infection risk, cover with sterile moist dressing.
  • Complicated: associated neurovascular injury.

4.2 Assessment – neurovascular (CSM)

Circulation: pulse, capillary refill, color, temperature distal to injury.
Sensation: ask about numbness, tingling; test light touch.
Motor: ability to move digits.

4.3 Splinting principles

  • Splint injury in position found (do not attempt to reduce).
  • Splint includes joint above and below.
  • Pad splint well (especially bony prominences).
  • Check neurovascular before and after splinting.
  • Improvised splints: cardboard, folded magazine, pillow (for ankle).

4.4 Specific dislocations

Shoulder: arm held slightly abducted, support in most comfortable position. Check axillary nerve (deltoid contraction, sensation over deltoid).

Finger: buddy tape to adjacent finger.

Knee: extremely high risk of vascular injury – check distal pulses meticulously; immobilize in position found.

4.5 Pelvic fracture

Suspect in high‑energy trauma, pain on palpation, or unstable pelvis. Bind pelvis with sheet or commercial pelvic binder to reduce volume and hemorrhage.

5. Burns thermal, chemical, electrical

5.1 Burn depth classification

  • Superficial (1st degree): erythema, pain, no blisters (e.g., sunburn).
  • Partial thickness (2nd degree): blisters, moist, very painful.
  • Full thickness (3rd degree): white/charred, leathery, painless (nerve destruction).

5.2 Rule of nines (adult)

  • Head & neck: 9%
  • Each arm: 9% (total 18%)
  • Anterior trunk: 18%
  • Posterior trunk: 18%
  • Each leg: 18% (total 36%)
  • Perineum: 1%

Child modification: head 18%, legs 14% each (relative to body proportions).

5.3 Immediate burn care

  • Stop burning: remove from heat source; smother flames (stop, drop, roll).
  • Cooling: cool running water (10‑20°C) for 20 minutes – within 3 hours post‑burn. Do not use ice (causes vasoconstriction, deepens injury).
  • Remove: clothing and jewelry (unless stuck).
  • Cover: cling film (plastic wrap) loosely – reduces pain, prevents contamination. Do not wrap circumferentially (risk of constriction).
  • Blisters: do not burst – intact blister protects from infection.
  • Hypothermia prevention: especially in large burns.

5.4 Chemical burns

  • Brush off dry powder (e.g., lime) before irrigation.
  • Irrigate copiously with water for at least 20 minutes (60 minutes for alkali).
  • Remove contaminated clothing carefully.

5.5 Electrical burns

  • Ensure power off before approaching.
  • Entry and exit wounds may be small, but deep tissue destruction (muscle, nerve) can be severe – monitor for cardiac arrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis.

5.6 When to refer to burn centre

Partial thickness >10% BSA, full thickness, face/hands/feet/genitalia/perineum, circumferential, inhalation injury, chemical/electrical.

6. Medical Emergencies recognition & first aid

6.1 Stroke – FAST‑H

Face: drooping
Arm: weakness
Speech: slurred/confused
Time: last known well
Hemorrhage: sudden severe headache (consider haemorrhagic).

Action: call EMS immediately, note time of onset. Keep patient NPO, lay on side if vomiting.

6.2 Seizure

  • Protect from injury (remove hazards, cushion head).
  • Do not restrain or put anything in mouth.
  • Time seizure – if >5 minutes or repeated without recovery, call EMS (status epilepticus).
  • After seizure (postictal), place in recovery position, check airway, reorient.

6.3 Anaphylaxis

Signs: sudden onset, urticaria, angioedema (lips/tongue), wheeze/stridor, hypotension, gastrointestinal symptoms.

First aid:

  • Call EMS immediately.
  • Epinephrine auto‑injector (IM): adult dose 0.3 mg, child 0.15 mg (if weight 15‑30 kg). Inject into mid‑anterolateral thigh (can be through clothing).
  • Repeat after 5‑15 minutes if no improvement.
  • Lay patient supine (or in position of comfort if respiratory distress); elevate legs if hypotensive.

6.4 Hypoglycemia (diabetic emergency)

Signs: altered mental status, diaphoresis, tachycardia, hunger, confusion, seizure.

If conscious and able to swallow: oral glucose (15‑20g) – glucose tablets, sugary drink, honey, candy.

If unconscious / unable to swallow: call EMS; place in recovery position; do not give oral anything. Glucagon injection (IM/subcut) if trained and available.

6.5 Heart attack (ACS)

Signs: chest pain/pressure (may radiate to jaw/arm), shortness of breath, nausea, diaphoresis. Atypical in women/elderly/diabetics.

Action: call EMS, have patient rest. If aspirin available and not allergic, give 160‑325 mg chewed. If nitroglycerin prescribed, assist.

6.6 Opioid overdose

Signs: unresponsive, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, cyanosis.

Action: call EMS, give naloxone (if available), start rescue breathing/CPR.
