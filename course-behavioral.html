1. Human Development Across the Lifespan theories & stages

1.1 Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Schemas, assimilation, accommodation, equilibration. Four stages:

  • Sensorimotor (0‑2 yrs): object permanence, goal‑directed action, circular reactions. Development of deferred imitation.
  • Preoperational (2‑7 yrs): symbolic thought, egocentrism (three‑mountains task), animism, centration, lack of conservation.
  • Concrete operational (7‑11 yrs): logical thinking, conservation, reversibility, classification, seriation. Still struggles with abstract concepts.
  • Formal operational (12+ yrs): abstract reasoning, hypothetical‑deductive thought, metacognition. Some adults remain at concrete level.

Nursing application: tailor communication to developmental level; assess for cognitive delays; educate parents about age‑appropriate stimulation.

1.3 Freud's Psychosexual Stages

  • Oral (0‑1): sucking, biting – fixation leads to oral behaviors (smoking, overeating).
  • Anal (1‑3): toilet training – fixation: anal‑retentive (perfectionist) or anal‑expulsive (messy).
  • Phallic (3‑6): Oedipus/Electra complex, identification with same‑sex parent.
  • Latency (6‑12): sexual impulses dormant, social skills develop.
  • Genital (12+): mature sexuality, relationships.

1.4 Kohlberg's Moral Development

Pre‑conventional: obedience (avoid punishment), self‑interest (what's in it for me).
Conventional: interpersonal conformity (good boy/girl), authority & social order (law and order).
Post‑conventional: social contract, universal ethical principles (justice, human rights).

2. Emotions & Learning Theories neurobiology & behavior

2.1 Theories of Emotion

  • James‑Lange theory: physiological response precedes emotion (we feel sad because we cry).
  • Cannon‑Bard theory: physiological and emotional responses occur simultaneously.
  • Schachter‑Singer (two‑factor): emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label.
  • Lazarus (cognitive‑mediational): appraisal of stimulus determines emotion.
  • Neurobiological basis: amygdala (fear, threat), prefrontal cortex (regulation), insula (interoception).

2.2 Classical Conditioning (Pavlov, Watson)

Unconditioned stimulus (UCS) → unconditioned response (UCR). Pair neutral stimulus with UCS → conditioned stimulus (CS) → conditioned response (CR).

Extinction, spontaneous recovery, generalization, discrimination. Example: conditioned nausea in chemotherapy patients.

2.3 Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

Reinforcement (increases behavior): positive (add reward), negative (remove aversive).
Punishment (decreases behavior): positive (add aversive), negative (remove reward).

Schedules: fixed ratio (high response rate), variable ratio (most resistant to extinction), fixed interval, variable interval.

2.4 Observational Learning (Bandura)

Attention, retention, reproduction, motivation. Bobo doll experiment. Social learning theory – modeling influences health behaviors (e.g., children imitate parents' smoking).

2.5 Health Belief Model & Transtheoretical Model

Health Belief Model: perceived susceptibility, severity, benefits, barriers, cues to action, self‑efficacy.

Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change): precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, termination. Relapse is common.

3. Personality Theories & Defense Mechanisms structure & dynamics

3.1 Trait Theories – Big Five (OCEAN)

  • Openness: imagination, curiosity vs conventional.
  • Conscientiousness: disciplined, organized vs careless.
  • Extraversion: sociable, energetic vs solitary.
  • Agreeableness: cooperative, compassionate vs antagonistic.
  • Neuroticism: anxious, moody vs emotionally stable (low neuroticism).

3.2 Freudian Structural Model

Id: unconscious, pleasure principle, primary process (wish fulfillment).
Ego: conscious, reality principle, secondary process (problem solving).
Superego: moral conscience, ideals (perfection).

4. Mental Health Disorders DSM‑5‑TR & nursing interventions

4.1 Anxiety Disorders

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD): excessive worry >6 months, restlessness, fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.

Panic disorder: recurrent unexpected panic attacks (palpitations, sweating, trembling, choking, depersonalization, fear of dying). Agoraphobia may develop.

Phobias: marked fear of specific object/situation (social anxiety, specific phobia).

Nursing interventions: stay calm, use grounding techniques (5‑4‑3‑2‑1), teach diaphragmatic breathing, cognitive restructuring, exposure therapy (graded).

4.2 Depressive Disorders

Major depressive disorder (MDD): ≥2 weeks of depressed mood or anhedonia plus 5+ symptoms (weight change, sleep disturbance, psychomotor changes, fatigue, worthlessness, poor concentration, suicidal ideation).

Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia): chronic low‑grade depression ≥2 years.

Nursing interventions: safety assessment (suicide risk), encourage activities of daily living, monitor nutrition/hydration, administer antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs), educate about delayed onset (2‑4 weeks).

4.3 Bipolar Disorders

Bipolar I: at least one manic episode (≥1 week, elevated/irritable mood, grandiosity, decreased sleep, pressured speech, flight of ideas, risk‑taking, psychotic features possible).

Bipolar II: hypomanic episodes (≥4 days, less severe) + major depressive episodes.

Nursing interventions: during mania – reduce stimuli, set limits, ensure safety (poor judgment), monitor sleep and nutrition, administer mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate, atypical antipsychotics). Monitor lithium levels (0.6‑1.2 mEq/L) and toxicity (tremor, ataxia, confusion).

4.4 Psychotic Disorders (Schizophrenia Spectrum)

Positive symptoms: hallucinations (auditory most common), delusions (fixed false beliefs – paranoid, grandiose, referential), disorganized speech (loose associations, word salad), disorganized/catatonic behavior.

Negative symptoms: flat affect, alogia (poverty of speech), avolition (lack of motivation), anhedonia, asociality.

Nursing interventions: establish therapeutic alliance, do not argue with delusions (focus on feelings), ensure medication adherence (antipsychotics – first generation: haloperidol, second generation: risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine for treatment‑resistant). Monitor for extrapyramidal symptoms (dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism) and tardive dyskinesia (AIMS assessment).

4.5 Trauma‑ & Stressor‑Related Disorders

PTSD: exposure to trauma, intrusive memories, nightmares, flashbacks, avoidance, negative cognitions, hyperarousal (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle).

Nursing interventions: trauma‑informed care (safety, trust, choice, collaboration, empowerment), grounding techniques, refer for trauma‑focused CBT or EMDR.

4.6 Personality Disorders (Cluster A, B, C)

Cluster A (odd/eccentric): paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal.
Cluster B (dramatic/erratic): antisocial, borderline (instability, fear of abandonment, self‑harm), histrionic, narcissistic.
Cluster C (anxious/fearful): avoidant, dependent, obsessive‑compulsive.

Borderline Personality Disorder – nursing: validate emotions, set consistent boundaries, dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) skills – mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation.

5. Sociology in Nursing social determinants & cultural competence

5.1 Family Structures & Dynamics

  • Nuclear: two parents + children.
  • Extended: multigenerational.
  • Single‑parent: one parent raising children.
  • Blended: remarried with children from previous relationships.
  • Cohabitating, same‑sex, communal.

Family systems theory: family as interconnected whole; change in one member affects all.

5.2 Cultural Competence & Humility

Leininger's Culture Care Theory: sunrise model – cultural care preservation/accommodation/repatterning.

Components: cultural awareness, knowledge, skill, encounter, desire. Avoid stereotyping. Assess explanatory model (Kleinman questions): "What do you think caused your illness?" "How do you think it should be treated?"

5.3 Social Determinants of Health (WHO)

  • Economic stability: employment, poverty, food insecurity.
  • Education: literacy, early childhood education.
  • Social and community context: social support, discrimination, civic participation.
  • Health and health care: access, health insurance, health literacy.
  • Neighborhood and built environment: housing, transportation, crime, environmental conditions.

Nursing role: screen for social needs (e.g., Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients' Assets, Risks, and Experiences – PRAPARE), refer to community resources, advocate for policy change.

6. Coping, Stress & Burnout Prevention resilience in nursing

6.1 Stress Models

Lazarus & Folkman Transactional Model: stress = person‑environment transaction. Primary appraisal (is this threat/harm/challenge?), secondary appraisal (can I cope?), reappraisal.

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome: alarm (fight or flight), resistance (coping), exhaustion (depletion, illness).

6.2 Coping Strategies

  • Problem‑focused: addressing the stressor directly (e.g., time management, seeking information).
  • Emotion‑focused: regulating emotional response (e.g., meditation, denial, venting).
  • Meaning‑focused: reappraising values/beliefs (e.g., finding positive meaning).
  • Social support: instrumental, informational, emotional.

6.3 Burnout in Healthcare

Three dimensions (Maslach):

  • Emotional exhaustion: feeling drained, depleted.
  • Depersonalization/cynicism: detached, negative attitudes toward patients.
  • Reduced personal accomplishment: feeling ineffective.

Causes: high workload, lack of control, insufficient reward, unfairness, values conflict, lack of community.

6.4 Resilience & Prevention Strategies

  • Individual: self‑care (sleep, exercise, nutrition), mindfulness, boundary setting, reflective practice, seeking supervision/peer support.
  • Organizational: manageable patient ratios, wellness programs, psychological safety, debriefing after critical events, positive leadership.

6.5 Compassion Fatigue & Secondary Traumatic Stress

Result of exposure to others' trauma. Symptoms similar to PTSD. Prevention: self‑awareness, professional boundaries, regular debriefing, therapy if needed.
