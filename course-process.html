1. Assessment — The Foundation

1.1 Data Collection: Subjective vs Objective

  • Subjective data: patient's verbal descriptions (symptoms). Only patient can provide. Examples: pain level, nausea, feelings of anxiety, "I feel like I can't breathe."
  • Objective data: measurable, observable, verifiable by others (signs). Examples: vital signs, lab values, physical exam findings (crackles, edema, wound appearance).

1.2 Sources of Data

  • Primary: the patient (most accurate, direct).
  • Secondary: family, caregivers, medical records, other healthcare team members, literature review.

1.3 Data Collection Methods — In Depth

Interview techniques

  • Open-ended questions: "Tell me what brought you to the hospital."
  • Closed-ended questions: "When did the pain start?" (used for specifics).
  • Therapeutic communication: active listening, silence, reflection, clarification, summarization.
  • Non-therapeutic: false reassurance, giving advice, changing the subject, using "why" (can sound accusatory).

Observation & physical examination

  • Inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation (see Advanced Health Assessment notes for full details).
  • Head-to-toe examination: systematic approach to avoid missing findings.

1.4 Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

A comprehensive assessment framework that organizes data into 11 patterns, making it easier to identify nursing diagnoses:

  1. Health perception–health management: perceived health, preventive practices, adherence.
  2. Nutritional–metabolic: diet, weight, skin condition, hydration.
  3. Elimination: bowel/bladder patterns, continence.
  4. Activity–exercise: ADLs, mobility, energy, respiratory/cardiovascular function.
  5. Sleep–rest: sleep quality, routines, fatigue.
  6. Cognitive–perceptual: pain, sensory deficits, cognition, language.
  7. Self-perception–self-concept: body image, self-esteem, emotional responses.
  8. Role–relationship: family roles, social interactions, communication.
  9. Sexuality–reproductive: reproductive health, sexual satisfaction, concerns.
  10. Coping–stress tolerance: stress levels, coping mechanisms, support systems.
  11. Value–belief: spiritual beliefs, values, life goals.

1.5 Documentation of Findings

  • Purpose: legal record, communication, reimbursement (ICD‑10 codes), research, quality improvement.
  • Formats: narrative, SOAP, DAR, electronic health record (EHR) with structured data entry.
  • Principles: timely, accurate, objective, complete, confidential (HIPAA).

Example: Admission Assessment Note
Subjective: "I've had this crushing chest pain for 2 hours, radiating to my jaw."
Objective: BP 150/92, HR 108, RR 22, SpO₂ 94% on RA. Diaphoretic. ECG: ST elevation in V2‑V4.
Functional pattern findings: Health perception: rates health as "poor." Activity: reports dyspnea on minimal exertion. Coping: anxious, states "I'm scared."

2. Nursing Diagnosis — Clinical Judgment

2.1 NANDA-I Taxonomy

Standardized terminology for nursing diagnoses. Updated every 3 years. Each diagnosis has a definition, defining characteristics (signs/symptoms), and related factors (etiologies).

2.2 Types of Nursing Diagnoses

TypeFormatExample
Actual3-part: Diagnostic label + related to + as evidenced by (R/T ... AEB)Ineffective Airway Clearance R/T retained secretions AEB coarse crackles, ineffective cough, tachypnea
Risk2-part: Risk for [diagnosis] + R/T risk factorsRisk for Falls R/T unsteady gait, age >80, orthostatic hypotension

SyndromeCluster of diagnoses that occur togetherPost‑Trauma Syndrome

2.3 Differentiating Nursing vs Medical Diagnosis

  • Medical diagnosis: identifies disease/condition (e.g., myocardial infarction, diabetes mellitus). Remains constant. Treated by physician/APRN.
  • Nursing diagnosis: describes human response to health conditions/life processes (e.g., Acute Pain, Decreased Cardiac Output). Can change as patient responds. Treated by nurses within scope.

2.4 Formulating a Nursing Diagnosis — Critical Thinking

  • Step 1: Cluster assessment data (find patterns).
  • Step 2: Identify patient needs/problems.
  • Step 3: Select NANDA label that fits.
  • Step 4: Identify related factors (etiology) — these guide nursing interventions.
  • Step 5: Document defining characteristics (AEB).

Example Case: Post-op day 1, patient refuses to ambulate, grimaces with movement, reports pain 7/10, vital signs: BP 160/88, HR 102.
Nursing diagnosis: Acute Pain R/T surgical incision AEB patient report of 7/10 pain, guarding behavior, tachycardia, and hypertension.

2.5 Common Errors in Diagnosis

  • Using a medical diagnosis as etiology (e.g., "Decreased Cardiac Output R/T MI" — too vague; better: R/T decreased contractility).
  • Using symptoms as etiology (circular).
  • Writing diagnosis as a need (e.g., "needs pain meds" — not a diagnosis).
  • Premature diagnosis without enough data.

3. Outcome Identification — Setting Goals

3.1 SMART Goals

  • Specific: clear, precise, focused.
  • Measurable: quantifiable or observable.
  • Attainable: realistic given patient/resources.
  • Relevant: meaningful to patient and diagnosis.
  • Time-bound: specific timeframe for achievement.

3.2 Short‑Term vs Long‑Term Goals

  • Short‑term: achievable in hours to days (often shift‑based). Example: "Patient will rate pain ≤3/10 within 2 hours of pain intervention."
  • Long‑term: weeks to months (post‑discharge). Example: "Patient will ambulate 100 feet without assistive device by discharge."

3.3 Involving the Patient in Goal Setting

  • Patient-centered care requires patient input. Goals should align with patient values and priorities.
  • Ask: "What is most important for you to achieve today?"
  • Negotiate realistic goals; don't impose.

3.4 NOC (Nursing Outcomes Classification)

Standardized outcomes with indicators and measurement scales (1‑5). Example: Pain Level (NOC code 2102) — indicators: reported pain, facial expression, restlessness. Rated from 1 (severe) to 5 (none).

Example Outcomes for Acute Pain:
- Patient will report pain ≤3/10 within 4 hours (SMART).
- Patient will demonstrate use of distraction techniques by end of shift.
- Patient will have stable vital signs (BP/HR within baseline) within 2 hours.

4. Planning — Designing Care

4.1 Prioritization Frameworks

  • ABCs: Airway, Breathing, Circulation — always first.
  • Maslow's Hierarchy: physiological needs (airway, breathing, circulation, nutrition, elimination) before safety, love/belonging, esteem, self‑actualization.
  • Urgency: life‑threatening vs non‑urgent.
  • Patient preference: what matters most to patient.

4.2 Selecting Nursing Interventions

  • Independent: nurse‑initiated without HCP order (positioning, teaching, comfort measures).
  • Dependent: require HCP order (medications, IV fluids, diagnostic tests).
  • Collaborative: require coordination with other disciplines (PT, OT, respiratory therapy, social work).

4.3 NIC (Nursing Interventions Classification)

Standardized interventions with activities. Example: Pain Management (NIC 1400) — activities: assess pain, administer analgesics, teach non‑pharmacological methods (distraction, imagery), position comfortably.

4.5 Collaboration & Referrals

  • Identify need for other disciplines (dietitian, wound care, case manager, chaplain).
  • Communication: SBAR, team huddles, interdisciplinary rounds.

5. Implementation — Putting Plan into Action

5.1 Types of Interventions (Reinforced)

  • Independent: oral care, repositioning, patient education, emotional support.
  • Dependent: administering medications, inserting Foley (if ordered), applying dressings per protocol.
  • Collaborative: coordinating PT, dietary modifications with food service, consulting wound care.

5.2 Health Teaching — Key Principles

  • Assess readiness to learn: pain, anxiety, language, health literacy.
  • Use teach‑back: "Tell me in your own words how you will take this medicine."
  • Tailor to learning style: visual (handouts), auditory (explain), kinesthetic (demonstrate).
  • Document teaching: what was taught, patient response, understanding.

5.3 Documentation During Implementation

  • DAR (Data, Action, Response):
    • Data: Patient reports pain 7/10, grimacing.
    • Action: Administered morphine 2mg IV, repositioned, taught deep breathing.
    • Response: 30 min later, pain 2/10, relaxed, vital signs stable.
  • SOAP (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan): more comprehensive.
  • Charting by exception (CBE): only abnormal findings, but must include parameters.

5.4 Delegation During Implementation

  • RN delegates to LPN (stable patients, routine tasks) and UAP (bathing, ambulating).
  • Supervise and evaluate: did UAP report abnormal vital signs? did LPN note changes?
  • Never delegate assessment, diagnosis, planning, evaluation, or teaching that requires nursing judgment.

6. Evaluation — Measuring Success

6.1 Reassessment & Goal Attainment

  • Evaluate outcomes: Did patient meet SMART goals? If yes, goal achieved. If partially, continue/modify. If no, revise plan.
  • Evaluate interventions: Were they effective? Should we try different ones?

6.2 Determining Goal Attainment — 3 Options

  1. Goal met: patient demonstrates behavior/response within timeframe. (e.g., pain ≤3/10).
  2. Goal partially met: some progress but not fully. (e.g., pain reduced to 5/10).
  3. Goal not met: no progress or deterioration.

6.3 Modifying the Plan of Care

  • If goal not met, reassess: Was diagnosis correct? Were interventions appropriate? Were outcomes realistic? Patient condition changed?
  • Revise: update diagnosis, outcomes, interventions based on new data.

6.4 Ongoing Process — Continuous Cycle

Evaluation leads back to assessment. The nursing process is a dynamic, circular, not linear, process. At every patient encounter, the nurse reassesses, re‑diagnoses, re‑plans, re‑implements, and re‑evaluates.

Example Evaluation Note:
Outcome: Pain ≤3/10 within 4 hours.
Evaluation: Goal met. Patient reports pain 2/10, relaxed, vital signs WNL. Continue current pain management plan.

Outcome: Ambulate in hallway with assist by end of shift.
Evaluation: Goal not met. Patient still reports dizziness upon standing. Orthostatic vitals: BP drop 30 mmHg. Notify provider, hold ambulation, reassess fluid status.
