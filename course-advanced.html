1. Advanced Health Assessment deep dive

1.1 Comprehensive health history – genomic & psychosocial layers

Biographical & contextual: age, gender identity, race/ethnicity, occupation, living situation, advance directives, health literacy, interpreter needs. Source of history (patient, family, records) and reliability assessment.

Chief complaint – verbatim, with duration. Example: “I feel like an elephant is sitting on my chest for the past hour.”

HPI – OLDCARTS & PQRST: Provocative/palliative, Quality, Region/radiation, Severity, Timing. Also associated symptoms (e.g., dyspnea, diaphoresis with chest pain).

Past medical history: Include childhood illnesses, accidents, blood transfusions, mental health history (PTSD, depression), substance use disorder, functional status (Karnofsky scale if oncology).

Medication reconciliation: Brown bag method. Check for duplicate therapy, high‑risk combinations (warfarin + NSAIDs), complementary/herbal interactions (St. John’s wort & SSRIs).

Family history – three‑generation genogram: document ages, causes of death, hereditary cancers (BRCA), cardiomyopathies, autoimmune clusters, and ethnic predisposition (Tay‑Sachs, sickle cell).

Social history – HEADSSS for adolescents: Home, Education/employment, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety. For adults: food security, housing stability, social support, intimate partner violence screening (HITS).

Review of systems – exhaustive: also include sleep (insomnia, OSA risk), fatigue, falls, frailty, skin integrity (pressure injury risk), and for elderly – Get‑Up‑and‑Go test.

1.2 Physical examination – advanced semiology

Inspection: skin turgor, capillary refill, mucous membranes, conjunctival pallor, nail clubbing, cyanosis, asymmetries, abnormal movements.

Palpation: light then deep. Assess for guarding, rigidity, rebound (peritonitis). Liver span (percuss lower & upper border). Spleen palpation (enlargement).

Percussion: Tympany (stomach), dullness (consolidation, mass), shifting dullness (ascites).

Auscultation: carotid bruits, abdominal bruits (renal artery stenosis), bowel sounds (hypoactive ileus vs hyperactive obstruction). Cardiac: S1, S2, physiologic splitting, S3 (heart failure), S4 (stiff ventricle), murmurs (grade 1‑6, radiation, maneuver changes).

1.3 Focused vs comprehensive – clinical reasoning

Comprehensive: annual wellness visit, admission to hospital, preoperative baseline.

Focused – hypothesis‑driven: e.g., acute dyspnea → focused lung (crackles, wheezes), cardiac (JVP, heave), lower extremity (DVT signs).

1.4 Special populations – nuanced

Pediatrics: New Ballard Score for gestational age; WHO growth velocity; developmental red flags (no babbling by 12 months). Use FLACC or NIPS for pain.

Geriatrics: Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) – cognition (SLUMS), gait speed, nutrition (MNA), polypharmacy (Beers Criteria 2023), frailty phenotype (Fried criteria).

Pregnancy: Bishop score (cervical ripeness); assessment for preterm labor (fetal fibronectin).

1.5 Documentation & medicolegal

SOAP / DAR / PIE: Use specific, measurable terms. Avoid “patient appears comfortable” – instead: “patient resting quietly, VS stable, denies pain.”

Legal: chain of custody for forensic cases; duty to warn (Tarasoff); mandated reporting (child/elder abuse).

2. Critical Care Nursing deep dive

2.1 Hemodynamic monitoring – advanced physics & waveforms

Transducer system: phlebostatic axis (4th ICS, midaxillary). Fast‑flush test: underdamping (overshoot) → false high systolic; overdamping (slurred) → false low systolic. Rezero after position changes.

Arterial line: dicrotic notch = aortic valve closure. Pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg drop in inspiration) suggests tamponade, COPD, asthma. Pulsus alternans (beat‑to‑beat variation) = severe heart failure.

CVP waveform: a wave (atrial contraction), c wave (tricuspid bulging), x descent, v wave (atrial filling), y descent. Cannon a waves in junctional rhythm; large v waves in tricuspid regurgitation. Kussmaul sign (JVP rises in inspiration) = constrictive pericarditis.

PA catheter waveforms: right atrium (2‑8 mmHg), right ventricle (15‑30/0‑8), pulmonary artery (15‑30/5‑13), PAOP (wedge) 6‑12 mmHg. Confirm wedge by appearance of atrial waveform and oxygen saturation ≥95%.

Derived parameters: SVR = [(MAP‑CVP)/CO]×80 (800‑1200). PVR = [(MPAP‑PAOP)/CO]×80 (<250). LVSWI, RVSWI. DO₂ = CO × (1.34×Hgb×SaO₂) (900‑1100 mL/min). VO₂ = CO × (1.34×Hgb×[SaO₂‑SvO₂]) (200‑250 mL/min). O₂ extraction ratio = (SaO₂‑SvO₂)/SaO₂ (norm 0.25).

Clinical integration: Low SvO₂ (<60%) = decreased DO₂ (anemia, hypoxemia, low CO) or increased VO₂ (shivering, fever, pain). High SvO₂ (>80%) = peripheral shunting (sepsis), cyanide poisoning, left‑to‑right shunt.

2.2 Mechanical ventilation – advanced modes & lung protection

Volume control vs pressure control: in volume control, guaranteed Vt but variable pressure (risk of barotrauma). Pressure control limits pressure but Vt varies with compliance.

Advanced modes: APRV (airway pressure release ventilation) – inverse ratio, recruits lung in ARDS. PRVC (pressure‑regulated volume control) – combines both. HFOV (high‑frequency oscillation) – used in severe ARDS, tiny tidal volumes.

ARDS protocol: low Vt (6 mL/kg PBW), plateau pressure ≤30, driving pressure (Pplat – PEEP) <15, permissive hypercapnia (pH >7.15). Prone positioning (improves V/Q matching).

Ventilator graphics: Flow‑volume loops (obstruction → concave expiratory). Pressure‑volume loops – lower inflection point (recruitment), upper inflection point (overdistension).

Weaning: Rapid shallow breathing index (RSBI) = f/Vt. RSBI <105 predicts successful extubation. Spontaneous breathing trial (T‑piece or PS 5‑8) for 30‑120 min.

2.3 Sepsis & septic shock – pathophysiology & bundles

Pathophysiology: Pathogen PAMPs bind TLRs → massive cytokine release (TNF‑α, IL‑1, IL‑6) → endothelial injury, microvascular thrombosis, vasodilation, myocardial suppression, mitochondrial dysfunction, cytopathic hypoxia.

Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2023: Hour‑1 bundle: lactate, blood cultures, broad antibiotics, 30 mL/kg crystalloid, vasopressors if MAP <65. Reassess lactate within 2‑4h (goal decrease ≥20%).

Fluid responsiveness: Passive leg raise (PLR) → increase in CO ≥10% predicts fluid responsiveness. Use dynamic indices (PPV, SVV) if on ventilator.

Vasopressor choice: Norepinephrine first‑line. Add vasopressin (0.03 U/min) if refractory. Epinephrine for anaphylaxis / severe hypotension. Dobutamine if low cardiac output with high filling pressures.

2.4 Neurocritical care – advanced ICP & multimodality monitoring

ICP waveform morphology: P1 (percussion) – arterial, P2 (tidal) – reflects brain compliance, P3 (dicrotic). Rising P2 indicates low compliance, high risk of herniation.

CPP = MAP – ICP. Target 60‑70. Autoregulation – if pressure passive, PRx (pressure reactivity index) positive → poor outcome.

Brain tissue oxygenation (PbtO₂): normal 20‑40 mmHg. <20 treat (optimize CPP, increase FiO₂, transfusion).

Cerebral microdialysis: lactate/pyruvate ratio >40 indicates metabolic crisis.

Herniation syndromes: uncal (ipsilateral pupil dilation, contralateral hemiparesis), central (bilateral pinpoint pupils, Cheyne‑Stokes), tonsillar (Cushing’s triad: hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations).

3. Advanced Pharmacology deep dive

3.1 Pharmacokinetics – detailed pathways

Absorption: Bioavailability (IV=1, oral <1). P‑glycoprotein efflux in gut limits absorption of some drugs (e.g., digoxin). Food interactions: grapefruit inhibits CYP3A4, increases statin/felodipine levels.

Distribution: Volume of distribution (Vd) = amount in body / plasma concentration. Highly lipophilic (e.g., fentanyl) large Vd. Protein binding: warfarin 99% bound, only free active. Displacement by sulfonamides can cause bleeding.

Metabolism – CYP450 isoenzymes: CYP3A4 (50% drugs – statins, CCBs, midazolam), CYP2D6 (antidepressants, codeine activation), CYP2C9 (warfarin, phenytoin). Genetic polymorphisms: poor vs ultra‑rapid metabolizers (codeine toxicity in ultrarapid).

Excretion – renal: Glomerular filtration, tubular secretion (penicillins), reabsorption (lithium – influenced by Na intake). Adjust for CrCl (Cockcroft‑Gault).

3.2 Pharmacodynamics – receptor theory

Agonist, antagonist, partial agonist, inverse agonist. Efficacy vs potency. Therapeutic index – narrow: warfarin, digoxin, lithium, theophylline, phenytoin.

Receptor types: G‑protein coupled (β‑agonists), ion channels (benzodiazepines), enzyme‑linked (insulin), intracellular (steroids).

3.3 High‑alert medications – error prevention

Insulin: U‑100 vs U‑500 (high risk). Never use “u” abbreviation. Independent double‑check before administration. Protocols for hypoglycemia (rule of 15).

Heparin: Weight‑based nomogram. Monitor aPTT or anti‑Xa. HIT type II (immune mediated) → stop heparin, start direct thrombin inhibitor (argatroban, bivalirudin).

Opioids: Respiratory depression risk. Equianalgesic dosing (morphine 10 mg IV = hydromorphone 1.5 mg IV). Naloxone infusion for long‑acting overdoses.

Vasopressors: Extravasation of norepinephrine → phentolamine infiltration. Central line preferred.

3.4 Antibiotic stewardship – de‑escalation & PK/PD

PK/PD indices: Time‑dependent (β‑lactams – time above MIC), concentration‑dependent (aminoglycosides, daptomycin – Cmax/MIC), AUC/MIC (vancomycin).

Therapeutic drug monitoring: vancomycin trough 15‑20 (for serious infections), peak for gentamicin/tobramycin (synergy dosing vs traditional).

4. Leadership & Management deep dive

4.1 Leadership theories & application

Transformational: idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration. Promotes shared governance.

Authentic leadership: self‑awareness, transparency, ethical/moral reasoning.

Complexity leadership: adaptive, enabling conditions for innovation in chaotic healthcare environments.

4.2 Delegation – five rights & legal scope

Right task: routine, non‑invasive, predictable outcome. Cannot delegate assessment, diagnosis, care planning, evaluation.

Right circumstance: stable patient, appropriate setting.

Right person: UAP vs LPN scope (LPN can reinforce teaching, monitor IV, but not initiate blood).

Right direction/communication: clear task, expected outcome, limitations, time frame.

Right supervision/evaluation: RN remains responsible for follow‑up and patient outcome.

4.3 Conflict resolution & negotiation

Styles: competing (emergency), collaborating (win‑win), compromising (moderate), avoiding (trivial), accommodating (relationship).

Interest‑based negotiation: separate people from problem, focus on interests not positions, invent options, use objective criteria.

4.4 Quality improvement – methodologies

PDSA: rapid cycle tests. Lean: eliminate waste (muda). Six Sigma: DMAIC (define, measure, analyze, improve, control).

Root cause analysis (RCA): after sentinel event – ask “why” five times, identify system vulnerabilities, implement action plan.

5. Evidence‑Based Practice deep dive

5.1 PICO(T) – building the question

Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Time. Example: In mechanically ventilated adults (P), does subglottic suctioning (I) compared to standard ETT care (C) reduce VAP incidence (O) within 14 days (T)?

5.2 Levels of evidence & GRADE

Hierarchy: systematic reviews / meta‑analysis, RCT, cohort, case‑control, cross‑sectional, case series, expert opinion. GRADE: high, moderate, low, very low – based on risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias.

5.3 Database searching – MeSH & Boolean

PubMed, CINAHL, Embase, Cochrane Library. Use MeSH terms: “Respiration, Artificial/adverse effects”, Boolean: (ventilator‑associated pneumonia OR VAP) AND prevention. Filter by publication type, years, language.

5.4 Critical appraisal – CASP & CONSORT

Check validity: randomization, allocation concealment, blinding, intention‑to‑treat. Assess results: effect size (RR, NNT), precision (CI). Apply to your population.

6. Ethics & Law deep dive

6.1 Ethical principles – applied cases

Autonomy: informed refusal, advanced directives, living wills, DNR/POLST. Beneficence: providing benefit, balancing risks. Non‑maleficence: do no harm – e.g., avoiding unnecessary restraints. Justice: equitable allocation (ICU beds during pandemic).

6.2 Informed consent – elements & exceptions

Elements: disclosure, comprehension, voluntariness, competence. Exceptions: emergency (implied consent), therapeutic privilege (rare), patient waiver. Nurse’s role: witness, clarify, advocate.

6.3 Negligence vs malpractice – four Ds

Duty: nurse‑patient relationship. Dereliction: breach of standard of care. Direct cause: breach caused injury. Damages: physical, financial, emotional harm. Example: failure to monitor post‑op bleeding → hypovolemic shock.

6.4 Legal doctrines & professional regulation

Respondeat superior: employer liable. Res ipsa loquitur: “the thing speaks for itself” (e.g., wrong site surgery). Statute of limitations: time to file claim (varies by state). Nurse Practice Acts define scope, delegation, and disciplinary actions.
