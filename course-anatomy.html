Respiratory · Reproductive · Urinary · Nervous · Endocrine · Digestive · Excretory

1. Respiratory System — Detailed Anatomy

1.1 Upper Respiratory Tract

Nasal Cavity: Divided by nasal septum; lined with ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium with goblet cells (mucus production). Functions: warms, filters, humidifies air. Conchae (superior, middle, inferior) increase surface area. Olfactory epithelium in superior region for smell.

Pharynx (throat): Three parts:

  • Nasopharynx: Behind nasal cavity; contains pharyngeal tonsils (adenoids) and openings of Eustachian tubes (equalize pressure).
  • Oropharynx: Behind mouth; contains palatine and lingual tonsils. Common passage for air, food, drink.
  • Laryngopharynx: Opens into esophagus and larynx; lined by stratified squamous epithelium (protection).

Larynx (voice box): Cartilaginous structure (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottis, arytenoids). Epiglottis prevents aspiration during swallowing. Vocal cords: true (produce sound) and false (vestibular). Innervation: recurrent laryngeal nerve (branch of vagus).

1.2 Lower Respiratory Tract

Trachea: 10-12 cm long, C-shaped cartilaginous rings (open posteriorly for esophageal expansion). Lined by ciliated epithelium and goblet cells (mucus escalator). Bifurcates at carina into right and left main bronchi.

Bronchial Tree:

  • Right main bronchus: Shorter, wider, more vertical – common site for aspiration.
  • Left main bronchus: Longer, narrower, more horizontal.
  • Branch into lobar (secondary) bronchi (3 right, 2 left) → segmental (tertiary) bronchi → bronchioles → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveolar sacs → alveoli.

Bronchioles: No cartilage; smooth muscle controls airflow (autonomic: sympathetic dilates, parasympathetic constricts).

1.3 Alveoli — Site of Gas Exchange

  • Type I pneumocytes: Thin squamous cells covering 95% of alveolar surface; site of gas diffusion.
  • Type II pneumocytes: Cuboidal, secrete pulmonary surfactant (reduces surface tension, prevents alveolar collapse). Proliferate after injury to regenerate epithelium.
  • Alveolar macrophages (dust cells): Phagocytose debris, pathogens.

Respiratory membrane: Alveolar epithelium → fused basement membrane → capillary endothelium. Thickness 0.5-1 μm; gases diffuse by partial pressure gradients.

1.4 Pleura & Lung Lobes

  • Visceral pleura: Covers lung surface.
  • Parietal pleura: Lines thoracic cavity.
  • Pleural cavity: Thin space with serous fluid (reduces friction).
  • Right lung: 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior), 10 segments.
  • Left lung: 2 lobes (superior, inferior), cardiac notch, lingula, 8-9 segments.

1.5 Blood Supply & Innervation

  • Pulmonary circulation: Deoxygenated blood from RV → pulmonary arteries → capillaries (gas exchange) → pulmonary veins → LA.
  • Bronchial circulation: Systemic supply to lung tissue (bronchial arteries from aorta).
  • Innervation: Pulmonary plexus (sympathetic: bronchodilation; parasympathetic: bronchoconstriction).
🫁 Clinical Correlation: Pneumothorax (air in pleural cavity) causes lung collapse. Surfactant deficiency (neonatal RDS) leads to alveolar collapse. COPD involves destruction of alveolar walls (emphysema) and chronic bronchitis.

2. Digestive System — From Mouth to Anus

2.1 Layers of GI Tract (from inside out)

  • Mucosa: Epithelium (varies by region), lamina propria (connective tissue), muscularis mucosae (smooth muscle).
  • Submucosa: Connective tissue with blood vessels, lymphatics, submucosal plexus (Meissner's).
  • Muscularis externa: Inner circular, outer longitudinal smooth muscle; myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) between layers.
  • Serosa / Adventitia: Outer covering (serosa if intraperitoneal, adventitia if retroperitoneal).

2.2 Mouth & Esophagus

Oral cavity: Lips, cheeks, hard/soft palate, tongue (papillae: filiform, fungiform, circumvallate), teeth (incisors, canines, premolars, molars). Salivary glands: parotid (serous), submandibular (mixed), sublingual (mucous). Saliva contains amylase, lysozyme, IgA.

Esophagus: 25 cm muscular tube; upper 1/3 skeletal muscle, middle mixed, lower 1/3 smooth muscle. Passes through esophageal hiatus (T10). Lower esophageal sphincter (LES) prevents reflux.

2.3 Stomach

Regions: cardia, fundus, body, antrum, pylorus. Rugae (folds) allow distension.

Cells of gastric glands:

  • Mucous neck cells: Mucus.
  • Chief cells: Pepsinogen (activated to pepsin by HCl).
  • Parietal cells: HCl (intrinsic factor for B12 absorption).
  • G cells: Gastrin (hormone stimulates acid secretion).
  • Enterochromaffin-like (ECL) cells: Histamine (stimulates parietal cells).

Gastric juice: 2-3 L/day, pH 1.5-3.5.

2.4 Small Intestine (6-7 m)

  • Duodenum: First 25 cm, retroperitoneal. Receives bile (common bile duct) and pancreatic juice via major duodenal papilla (hepatopancreatic ampulla of Vater).
  • Jejunum: Upper 2/5 of remaining; thick wall, many folds (plicae circulares).
  • Ileum: Lower 3/5; thinner, Peyers patches (lymphoid tissue), absorbs B12 and bile salts.

Structural adaptations for absorption: Plicae circulares, villi (finger-like projections), microvilli (brush border). Each villus contains capillary network and lacteal (lymphatic).

2.5 Large Intestine (1.5 m)

Parts: cecum (appendix attached), ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid colon, rectum, anal canal. Taeniae coli (three bands of longitudinal muscle), haustra (pouches), appendices epiploicae (fat tags).

Functions: Water absorption (900 mL → 100 mL), electrolyte absorption, formation of feces, bacterial flora produce vitamin K and some B vitamins.

2.6 Accessory Organs

  • Liver: Largest gland (1.5 kg). Lobules with hepatocytes, portal triads (hepatic artery, portal vein, bile duct). Produces bile (500-1000 mL/day). Stores glycogen, synthesizes proteins, detoxifies.
  • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile. Contracts in response to CCK.
  • Pancreas: Exocrine (acini secrete digestive enzymes: trypsin, chymotrypsin, lipase, amylase) and endocrine (islets of Langerhans: insulin β, glucagon α, somatostatin δ).
🩺 Clinical: Peptic ulcers (H. pylori, NSAIDs). Appendicitis (inflammation of appendix). Cirrhosis (liver fibrosis). Pancreatitis (autodigestion by enzymes). Celiac disease (villous atrophy from gluten).

3. Nervous System — CNS & PNS

3.1 Central Nervous System (CNS)

Brain: ~100 billion neurons. Divided into:

  • Cerebrum: Two hemispheres (corpus callosum connects). Lobes: frontal (motor, executive), parietal (somatosensory), temporal (auditory, memory), occipital (visual). Cortex: gray matter (6 layers); white matter (tracts). Basal ganglia (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus) – motor control.
  • Diencephalon: Thalamus (relay station), hypothalamus (homeostasis, pituitary control), epithalamus (pineal gland – melatonin).
  • Brainstem: Midbrain (reflexes, eye movement), pons (bridge, respiratory), medulla oblongata (vital centers: cardiac, respiratory, vasomotor).
  • Cerebellum: Coordination, balance, fine motor control.

Spinal cord: 31 segments (8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal). Gray matter (anterior horn – motor; posterior horn – sensory; lateral horn – autonomic). White matter tracts (ascending sensory, descending motor).

3.2 Meninges & CSF

  • Dura mater: Tough outer layer; forms dural folds (falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli).
  • Arachnoid mater: Middle layer; subarachnoid space contains CSF.
  • Pia mater: Delicate inner layer adheres to brain/spinal cord.
  • CSF: Produced by choroid plexus in ventricles; circulates and cushions; 150 mL total, replaced 3x daily.

3.4 Autonomic Nervous System

  • Sympathetic: Thoracolumbar (T1-L2). Neurotransmitter: norepinephrine (postganglionic). Fight or flight: increased HR, bronchodilation, decreased digestion.
  • Parasympathetic: Craniosacral (CN III, VII, IX, X; S2-4). Neurotransmitter: acetylcholine. Rest and digest: decreased HR, increased GI motility.
  • Enteric: Intrinsic nerve plexus in gut (can function independently).
🧠 Clinical: Stroke (CVA) – deficits correspond to affected artery (MCA: face/arm weakness). Parkinson's (dopamine loss in substantia nigra). Multiple sclerosis (demyelination). Herniated disc compressing spinal nerve.

4. Endocrine System — Hormonal Control

4.1 Hypothalamus & Pituitary

Hypothalamus: Master regulator; produces releasing/inhibiting hormones that control anterior pituitary. Also produces ADH and oxytocin (stored in posterior pituitary).

Pituitary gland (hypophysis):

  • Anterior (adenohypophysis): GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, prolactin. Regulated by hypothalamic hormones via hypophyseal portal system.
  • Posterior (neurohypophysis): Releases ADH (vasopressin) – increases water reabsorption in kidneys; oxytocin – uterine contraction, milk ejection.

4.2 Thyroid & Parathyroid

Thyroid gland: Butterfly shape in neck. Follicles store colloid (thyroglobulin). Produces T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine) – increase metabolism. Parafollicular (C) cells produce calcitonin (lowers blood Ca²⁺).

Parathyroid glands: 4 small glands on posterior thyroid. Produce PTH (parathyroid hormone) – increases blood Ca²⁺ by bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, activating vitamin D.

4.3 Adrenal Glands

Located on top of kidneys. Two regions:

  • Cortex (outer): Zona glomerulosa (aldosterone – Na⁺ retention, K⁺ excretion), zona fasciculata (cortisol – stress, metabolism), zona reticularis (androgens).
  • Medulla (inner): Chromaffin cells secrete epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%) – sympathetic response.

4.4 Pancreas (Endocrine)

  • Islets of Langerhans: β cells (insulin – lowers blood glucose), α cells (glucagon – raises glucose), δ cells (somatostatin – inhibits both), PP cells (pancreatic polypeptide).

4.5 Other Endocrine Glands

  • Pineal gland: Melatonin – circadian rhythm.
  • Gonads: Testes (testosterone), ovaries (estrogen, progesterone).
  • Thymus: Thymosin – T lymphocyte maturation (shrinks after puberty).
  • Kidney: Renin, erythropoietin (EPO).
  • Heart: Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) – reduces BP.

💊 Clinical: Diabetes mellitus (insulin deficiency/resistance). Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease). Cushing's syndrome (excess cortisol). Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency).

5. Urinary (Excretory) System — Kidneys & Nephrons

5.1 Kidney Gross Anatomy

Paired retroperitoneal organs (T12-L3). Hilum: renal artery, vein, ureter. Layers: renal capsule, cortex (outer), medulla (inner pyramids), renal pelvis (funnel to ureter). Calyces (minor → major) collect urine.

5.2 Nephron — Functional Unit

Each kidney ~1 million nephrons. Two types: cortical (85%) and juxtamedullary (15% – long loops for concentration).

  • Renal corpuscle: Glomerulus (capillary tuft) + Bowman's capsule. Filtration membrane: fenestrated endothelium, basement membrane, podocyte foot processes (slit diaphragms).
  • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): Cuboidal cells with microvilli (brush border). Reabsorbs 65% Na⁺, 100% glucose, amino acids, 65% water, HCO₃⁻. Secretes H⁺, NH₃, drugs.
  • Loop of Henle: Descending thin limb (water permeable, reabsorbs water), ascending thick limb (Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ cotransport, impermeable to water) – creates medullary osmotic gradient.
  • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT): Reabsorbs Na⁺ (regulated by aldosterone), secretes K⁺, H⁺. Calcium reabsorption regulated by PTH.
  • Collecting duct: Principal cells (Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ secretion under aldosterone; water reabsorption under ADH), intercalated cells (acid-base).

5.3 Urine Formation Steps

  1. Glomerular filtration: 120 mL/min (180 L/day). Filtrate: water, ions, glucose, urea, etc. No cells, no large proteins.
  2. Tubular reabsorption: 99% of filtrate reabsorbed. 1.5 L urine/day.
  3. Tubular secretion: Active transport of wastes (H⁺, K⁺, drugs) from blood into tubule.

5.4 Juxtaglomerular Apparatus & RAAS

  • Juxtaglomerular cells: Modified smooth muscle in afferent arteriole – secrete renin in response to low BP, sympathetic stimulation.
  • Macula densa: DCT cells sensing NaCl – signal JG cells.
  • Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS): Renin → angiotensin I → ACE (lungs) → angiotensin II → vasoconstriction, aldosterone release, ADH release, thirst → BP up.

5.5 Ureters, Bladder, Urethra

  • Ureters: 25-30 cm long, propel urine by peristalsis. Enter bladder obliquely (prevents reflux).
  • Urinary bladder: Detrusor muscle (smooth). Trigone (triangle between ureters and urethra). Internal urethral sphincter (smooth muscle, autonomic), external urethral sphincter (skeletal, voluntary).
  • Urethra: Male: 20 cm, prostatic, membranous, penile. Female: 4 cm, external urethral orifice.
💧 Clinical: UTI (cystitis, pyelonephritis). Renal calculi (kidney stones). Acute kidney injury (prerenal, renal, postrenal). Diabetic nephropathy. Dialysis for renal failure.

6. Reproductive System — Male & Female

6.1 Male Reproductive Anatomy

  • Testes: In scrotum (kept 3°C cooler). Tunica albuginea. Seminiferous tubules (spermatogenesis – Sertoli cells support). Interstitial cells (Leydig) produce testosterone.
  • Epididymis: Head, body, tail on testis; sperm mature and stored (20 days).
  • Vas (ductus) deferens: 45 cm, passes through inguinal canal, joins seminal vesicle to form ejaculatory duct.
  • Accessory glands:
    • Seminal vesicles: Produce 60% semen volume – fructose (energy for sperm), prostaglandins, clotting proteins.
    • Prostate: Surrounds urethra; produces thin, milky, alkaline fluid (neutralizes vaginal acidity).
    • Bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands: Mucus for lubrication, pre-ejaculate.
  • Penis: Root, body, glans. Corpora cavernosa (2) and corpus spongiosum (1) – erectile tissue.

6.2 Female Reproductive Anatomy

  • Ovaries: Cortex contains follicles at various stages (primordial to Graafian). Oogenesis: 1 ovum/month. Produce estrogen (granulosa cells) and progesterone (corpus luteum).
  • Fallopian tubes (uterine tubes): Fimbriae capture ovum. Site of fertilization (usually ampulla). Cilia and peristalsis move ovum/zygote.
  • Uterus: Pear-shaped, 7-8 cm. Layers: perimetrium (outer), myometrium (thick smooth muscle), endometrium (functional layer shed in menstruation, basal layer regenerates).
  • Cervix: Lower part, opens into vagina. Cervical mucus changes with cycle.
  • Vagina: 8-10 cm, birth canal, acidic (lactobacilli). Hymen (thin membrane).
  • External genitalia (vulva): Mons pubis, labia majora/minora, clitoris, vestibule, Bartholin's glands (mucus).
  • Mammary glands: Modified sweat glands; lobules (alveoli) produce milk; lactiferous ducts open at nipple.

6.4 Gametogenesis

  • Spermatogenesis: In seminiferous tubules; spermatogonia (2n) → primary spermatocyte (2n) → meiosis I → secondary spermatocyte (n) → meiosis II → spermatids (n) → sperm (spermiogenesis). Takes 64 days. Millions daily.
  • Oogenesis: Begins in fetal life; primary oocytes arrested in prophase I until puberty. Each month one completes meiosis I → secondary oocyte + polar body. Meiosis II completed only if fertilized.
👶 Clinical: BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia). Testicular torsion. PID (pelvic inflammatory disease). Endometriosis. Breast cancer screening. Infertility investigations.

7. Excretory System — Waste Removal

Note: The urinary system is the primary excretory route. Other excretory organs include:

  • Skin: Sweat glands excrete water, salts, urea (minor).
  • Lungs: Excrete CO₂, water vapor.
  • Liver: Excretes bile pigments (bilirubin) and toxins into feces.
  • Large intestine: Excretes undigested fiber, bacteria, some ions.

7.1 Urea Cycle & Nitrogen Excretion

Protein metabolism produces ammonia (toxic). Liver converts ammonia to urea (ornithine cycle). Urea travels via blood to kidneys for excretion.

7.2 Acid-Base Balance by Kidneys

Kidneys regulate blood pH by excreting H⁺ and reabsorbing HCO₃⁻. In acidosis: more H⁺ excreted as NH₄⁺, titratable acids; more HCO₃⁻ reabsorbed.

🧪 Clinical: Uremia (elevated urea in renal failure). Hepatic encephalopathy (failure to detoxify ammonia). Sweat chloride test for cystic fibrosis.
