1. Types of Abuse — Detailed Classification

1.1 Physical Abuse

  • Definition: Deliberate infliction of physical injury or harm. Includes hitting, shaking, throwing, poisoning, burning, drowning, suffocating, or fabricated/induced illness (formerly Munchausen by proxy).
  • Specific injury patterns:
    • Bruises: In non-mobile infants (any bruise is suspicious). Location: buttocks, back, genitals, face, ears, neck — soft tissue areas. Shape: handprint, belt mark, grip marks. Colour: inconsistent with history (different ages suggest repeated injury).
    • Fractures: In infants under 2 years, any fracture is suspicious unless major accidental trauma explained. Classic metaphyseal lesions (bucket handle fractures) — pathognomonic for abuse. Rib fractures (especially posterior) — caused by squeezing chest. Spiral fractures in non-ambulant child.
    • Burns: Immersion burns (stocking/glove pattern, clear demarcation). Cigarette burns (circular, 7-10mm, deep). Contact burns (shape of object — iron, grill). Scalds with symmetrical distribution.
    • Head trauma (Abusive Head Trauma — AHT): Previously "shaken baby syndrome." Triad: subdural haematoma, retinal haemorrhages, encephalopathy. No history of trauma or inconsistent history. May present with irritability, seizures, apnoea, bulging fontanelle.
    • Bite marks: Human bite marks — inter-canine distance >3cm suggests adult.

1.2 Emotional Abuse

  • Definition: Persistent emotional maltreatment that adversely affects child's emotional development. May involve:
  • Conveying worthlessness, unloved, inadequate.
  • Imposing inappropriate expectations (beyond developmental age).
  • Causing child to feel frightened or in danger (witnessing domestic abuse — see Topic 5).
  • Exploitation or corruption (involving child in criminal activity).
  • Indicators: Severe attachment disorders, developmental delay, extreme behaviour (withdrawn, aggressive, attention-seeking), self-harm, low self-esteem.

1.3 Sexual Abuse

  • Definition: Forcing or enticing a child to take part in sexual activities, whether or not child is aware of what is happening. Includes:
  • Penetrative acts (rape, buggery, oral sex).
  • Non-penetrative acts (touching, masturbation, kissing).
  • Non-contact (grooming, exposing child to pornography, indecent images, online abuse).
  • Child sexual exploitation (CSE) — see Topic 5.
  • Indicators: Disclosure (direct or indirect). Sexualised behaviour inappropriate for age. Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) in prepubertal child. Pregnancy. Genital/anal injury (bruising, bleeding, laxity). Enuresis, encopresis. Sudden changes in behaviour, self-harm, suicide attempts.

1.4 Neglect — The Most Common Form

  • Definition: Persistent failure to meet child's basic physical and/or psychological needs, likely to result in serious impairment of health or development.
  • Types:
    • Physical neglect: Failure to provide adequate food, shelter, clothing, supervision.
    • Medical neglect: Failure to seek/delay healthcare, missed immunisations, untreated chronic conditions.
    • Educational neglect: Failure to enrol child in school, persistent truancy.
    • Emotional neglect: Failure to provide nurture, affection, attention.
  • Indicators: Poor growth (failure to thrive), malnutrition, constant hunger, poor hygiene (unwashed, dirty clothes), untreated medical/dental issues, developmental delay, frequent school absence, child left unsupervised.

1.5 Fabricated or Induced Illness (FII)

  • Parent (usually mother) fabricates symptoms or deliberately induces illness (e.g., poisoning, suffocation, adding blood to urine).
  • Child subjected to numerous unnecessary medical procedures.
  • Red flags: Symptoms only reported by parent, not observed; child improves away from parent; parent medically knowledgeable but rejects reassurance; history of unexplained illnesses/sibling death.

Key Principle: Abuse types often co-exist. A child experiencing physical abuse is also likely experiencing emotional abuse and possibly neglect.

2. Recognition & Red Flags — Clinical Decision-Making

2.1 Inconsistent or Changing History

  • History does not explain injury (e.g., "fell off bed" but has multiple rib fractures).
  • History changes over time or differs between caregivers.
  • Delay in seeking medical attention (hours/days) without plausible explanation.
  • Child's developmental stage inconsistent with injury (e.g., non-mobile infant with fracture).

2.2 Patterned Injuries — Forensically Significant

  • Bruises: Petechiae, linear marks, clustered, shape of implement.
  • Fractures: Multiple fractures at different healing stages. Fractures in non-mobile infant. Posterior rib fractures, scapular fractures, sternal fractures.
  • Burns: Clear lines of demarcation (immersion), no splash marks, symmetrical.

2.3 Behavioural Indicators in Child

  • Fearful of parents/carers (flinching, avoiding).
  • Withdrawn, frozen watchfulness (hypervigilance).
  • Aggressive, acting out (may model abusive behaviour).
  • Overly compliant, eager to please.
  • Reluctant to go home.
  • Sexualised behaviour inappropriate for age.
  • Self-harm, suicidal ideation.

2.4 Parent/Carer Red Flags

  • Hostile, aggressive, defensive when asked about injury.
  • Blames child or third party.
  • Disinterested in child, minimal interaction.
  • History of domestic abuse, substance misuse, mental illness.
  • Previous children removed or known to social care.
  • Unrealistic expectations of child (beyond developmental stage).

2.5 Environmental Red Flags

  • Home conditions: unsafe, unsanitary, no food, exposure to drugs/alcohol.
  • Isolation: child not allowed to attend school, not registered with GP.
  • Frequent moves, avoiding services.

Remember: Any injury in a pre-mobile infant = child protection concern until proven otherwise. Document all findings with body diagrams and photographs (with consent/policy).

3. Communication & Forensic Interviewing

3.1 Principles of Communicating with Children

  • Age-appropriate language: Use simple words, short sentences. For young children, use concrete terms (e.g., "tummy" not "abdomen").
  • Non-leading questions: Avoid suggesting answers. Instead of "Did Daddy touch you there?", ask "Is there anything you want to tell me about?" or "Tell me what happened."
  • Open-ended: "And then what happened?" "Tell me more about that."
  • Active listening: Show you are listening (nodding, reflecting).
  • Patience: Child may need time to trust, may disclose gradually.
  • Use of drawings/toys: "Can you draw what happened?" "Show me on the doll where you were hurt." (Note: can be leading if not done carefully — follow local protocol).

3.2 Forensic Interviewing — Structured Approach

In many jurisdictions, formal forensic interviews are conducted by specially trained professionals. However, first responders (nurses) may need to gather initial information.

  • PEACE model: Preparation & Planning, Engage & Explain, Account, Closure, Evaluate.
  • Memorandum of Good Practice (UK): guidance for interviewing children.
  • Four stages of interview:
    1. Rapport building: Neutral topics, establish trust.
    2. Free narrative: "Tell me everything you remember."
    3. Questioning: Open-ended → specific (if needed) → closed (avoid unless essential).
    4. Closure: Thank child, explain next steps (in age-appropriate way).

3.3 Documentation — Verbatim and Objective

  • Record exact words child used. Use quotation marks.
  • Avoid interpretation: "child seemed sad" — instead: "child cried, said 'I'm sad'."
  • Record who was present, time, location.
  • Include any non-verbal communication (pointing, nodding, facial expressions).
  • Document actions taken (who you informed, advice given).

Example of verbatim documentation:
At 14:30, during wound care, child said (pointing to father): "He hit me with the stick because I was bad." Child then became quiet and turned away. Father, who was present, denied this. I replied, "Tell me more about that." Child shook head and would not speak further. I informed safeguarding lead Dr. Jones at 14:45.

3.4 Do's and Don'ts

  • DO: Stay calm, reassure child they are not in trouble, use simple language, explain you may need to tell someone to keep them safe.
  • DON'T: Promise secrecy ("I won't tell anyone" — you must share with safeguarding). DON'T ask leading questions. DON'T show shock or disgust.

4. Reporting & Legislation — Legal Duties

4.1 Mandatory Reporting — Who, When, How

  • Who is mandated? All healthcare professionals (nurses, doctors, midwives), teachers, social workers, police. Some jurisdictions have specific laws; in UK, everyone has duty to safeguard.
  • Threshold for reporting: Reasonable suspicion, concern, or disclosure. You do not need proof.
  • To whom? Usually: designated safeguarding lead in organisation (e.g., named nurse/doctor). Then referral to children's social care (social services) and/or police.
  • Timing: Immediately (same day). If child at immediate risk, stay with child and call emergency services (police).

4.2 Key Legislation (UK Context)

  • Children Act 1989 & 2004: Paramountcy principle — child's welfare is paramount. Duty of local authority to investigate.
  • Working Together to Safeguard Children (2018): Statutory guidance for inter-agency working.
  • Education Act 2002: Duty on schools to safeguard.
  • Sexual Offences Act 2003: Defines child sex offences, grooming.
  • Serious Crime Act 2015: Introduced offence of domestic abuse in front of child.
  • Data Protection Act 2018 / GDPR: Allows sharing information without consent if safeguarding child.

4.3 Reporting Process Flow

  1. Immediate concern: If child in danger, call police (999). If not immediate, speak to safeguarding lead.
  2. Referral to children's social care: Usually by safeguarding lead. Includes: child's details, family, nature of concern, any previous concerns, action taken.
  3. Feedback: Social care must acknowledge referral within 1 working day and decide next steps (strategy discussion, section 47 enquiry).
  4. Record keeping: Keep detailed notes of all communications.

4.4 Confidentiality vs Safeguarding

  • Duty of confidentiality is not absolute. If child at risk, you MUST share information (with or without consent).
  • If parent refuses consent, you can still refer — inform them you are doing so (unless that would put child at greater risk).
  • Seek advice from safeguarding lead if unsure.

Remember: Failure to report a concern can have serious consequences — for the child and potentially your professional registration (NMC Fitness to Practise).

5. Domestic Violence, Grooming & Exploitation

5.1 Impact of Witnessing Domestic Abuse (DA)

  • Children who witness DA are at risk of significant emotional harm, even if not directly abused themselves.
  • Effects: Anxiety, depression, PTSD, aggression, sleep disturbance, developmental regression, school difficulties, normalization of violence.
  • Legal recognition: In UK, children exposed to DA are recognized as victims under the Domestic Abuse Act 2021.
  • Practice: Ask about DA routinely (e.g., using routine enquiry in antenatal, mental health, A&E settings).

5.2 Grooming Behaviours

  • Definition: Process by which an abuser builds trust with child (and sometimes family) to gain access and reduce likelihood of disclosure.
  • Stages:
    1. Targeting: Identifying vulnerable child (low self-esteem, isolated, neglected).
    2. Gaining trust: Offering gifts, attention, friendship, treats.
    3. Filling a need: Becoming confidante, replacing missing parental role.
    4. Isolating: Cutting child off from other support.
    5. Sexualising: Introducing sexual content gradually (jokes, pornography, touch).
    6. Maintaining control: Secrecy, threats, blackmail, guilt.
  • Online grooming: Social media, gaming platforms, messaging apps. Abuser may pretend to be child's age.

5.3 Child Sexual Exploitation (CSE)

  • Definition: Form of sexual abuse where child receives something (gifts, money, affection, status) in exchange for sexual activity.
  • Indicators: Going missing from home, older boyfriend/girlfriend, secretive phone use, unexplained gifts, substance misuse, sexually transmitted infections, self-harm.
  • Child Criminal Exploitation (CCE): "County lines" — children used to transport drugs, money. Often involves violence and intimidation.

5.4 Online Risks

  • Exposure to inappropriate content (pornography, violence).
  • Cyberbullying.
  • Grooming via social media.
  • Sexting (youth-produced sexual images) — can lead to sharing without consent, blackmail.
  • Radicalisation (exposure to extremist content).

Practice point: Ask young people about online activity, friends, and whether anyone has asked them to do something they felt uncomfortable with. Use tools like "Thinkuknow" (CEOP) for age-appropriate resources.

6. Safeguarding Procedures — Multi‑Agency Working

6.1 Multi‑Agency Working

  • Safeguarding is everyone's responsibility. Effective working requires collaboration between health, social care, police, education, youth justice, voluntary sector.
  • Information sharing: Key to early intervention. If in doubt, share (with purpose of safeguarding).
  • Local Safeguarding Children Partnership (LSCP): Replaced Local Safeguarding Children Boards — brings together statutory partners.

6.2 Child Protection Conferences

  • Initial Child Protection Conference (ICPC): Convened by local authority when concerns reach threshold. Multi‑agency meeting to share information and decide if child needs a Child Protection Plan.
  • Attendees: Parents, child (if appropriate), social worker, health visitor, school nurse, GP, police, probation, etc.
  • Outcomes:
    • Child Protection Plan: If child at risk of significant harm. Categories: physical abuse, emotional abuse, sexual abuse, neglect. Named lead social worker, core group meetings.
    • Child in Need (Section 17): Lower level of need, support provided.
    • No further action.
  • Review conferences: Held within 3 months, then every 6 months until plan ends.

6.3 Looked‑After Children (LAC)

  • Children in care of local authority (foster care, residential home, kinship care).
  • Often have experienced abuse/neglect. Need advocate.
  • Health assessments: Initial, then annual. Carried out by designated LAC nurse/doctor.
  • Care plans: Include health, education, placement stability.
  • Independent Reviewing Officer (IRO): Monitors child's case.

6.4 Role of the Nurse in Safeguarding

  • Identify: Recognise signs and red flags.
  • Respond: Listen, reassure, document.
  • Refer: To safeguarding lead / social care.
  • Record: Detailed, factual notes.
  • Attend meetings: Provide reports, attend conferences.
  • Support: Provide ongoing care to child and family (non-judgemental).
  • Prevent: Health promotion, parenting support, early intervention.

6.5 Allegations Against Staff

  • If concern about colleague's behaviour towards child, report to designated safeguarding lead (or LADO — Local Authority Designated Officer).
  • Do not investigate yourself.
